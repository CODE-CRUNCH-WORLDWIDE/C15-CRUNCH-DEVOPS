# Challenges — Week 10

Two challenges. Each is more open-ended than an exercise; each asks you to compose the week's tools into a policy or an investigation. Allow ~1 hour per challenge.

| Challenge | Topic                                                         | Difficulty |
|----------:|---------------------------------------------------------------|-----------:|
|        01 | Block unsigned images with a Kyverno admission policy         |     Medium |
|        02 | Trace a supply-chain attack back through the build provenance |       Hard |

---

## How challenges differ from exercises

Exercises walk you through a tool. Challenges drop you into a scenario and ask you to *compose* tools. They are deliberately more open-ended: the steps are not all spelled out, and more than one correct answer often exists. The grading rubric at the bottom of each challenge tells you what evidence to capture.

Challenges assume you have done the exercises. Do not skip ahead.

## What to submit

For each challenge:

- The YAML, shell, or code you produced.
- A screenshot or terminal capture of the working state.
- A short written reflection (the questions are at the end of each challenge file).

Submit alongside your homework. The challenges are graded on whether you produced working artifacts *and* whether the reflection demonstrates understanding of what you produced.

## A note on the open-ended parts

Both challenges include questions of the form "what would you do if X". There is no single right answer to these. The grading rewards reasoning over recall. If your answer is "I would use tool Y because of trade-off Z", and the reasoning is sound, the answer is acceptable even if a different student picks a different tool.

This shift — from "do this" to "decide what to do" — is the shift you make on the way from a competent operator to a senior one. Challenges are deliberately the place where that shift happens.
